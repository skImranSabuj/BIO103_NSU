Biological classification o The grouping of similar things for a specific purpose is  called  classification. o In biology, classification is the process of  arranging  organisms into groups based on similar characteristics . o The science of naming and classifying organisms is called  taxonomy .  o The term is derived from the  Greek taxis (ΓÇ£arrangement ΓÇ¥)  and  nomos (ΓÇ£law ΓÇ¥). o Taxonomists classify organisms into a structural hierarchy ΓÇö a multi - level system in which each group is nested, or  contained, within a larger group. 
---PAGE---
Early Biological classification o The earliest humans probably classified  organisms as  plants and animals.  o They may have further classified  plants as edible or  poisonous , and the  animals as harmful or harmless . o The first serious attempt was made  at 300 BC  to classify all  the organisms by the Greek philosopher and scientist,  Aristotle and his students . 
---PAGE---
AristotleΓÇÖs Biological classification o Aristotle and his students first classified the  organisms as  plant or animal.  o They then  classified the animals  according to  where they  lived,  resulted in three groupings : air animals, water  animals, and land animals .  o They classified the  plants according to the structure of  stems.  o Those with  soft stems were called herbs ; those with a  single woody stem were called trees ; and those with  many small woody stems were called shrubs . 
---PAGE---
AristotleΓÇÖs Biological classification 
---PAGE---
AristotleΓÇÖs Biological classification o AristotleΓÇÖs classification  system  survived for almost two  thousand years.  o However, by the beginning of the 18th century, over 10,000  kinds of organisms were known and AristotleΓÇÖs system was  unable to classify them all.  o Many of  newly discovered organisms would not fit into any  category of AristotleΓÇÖs simple system . 
---PAGE---
Modern Biological Classification o Carolus Linnaeus , a Swedish botanist, developed a simple  classification system that  forms the basis of our modern  method of classificatio n.  o At the start of the 18 th   century about 10,000 kinds of  organisms were known. By the end of that century over  70,000 kinds were known.  o Linnaeus tried to develop a classification system for this  large number of organisms.  o By 1753  his system was well developed and  modern  taxonomy began. 
---PAGE---
Carolus Linnaeus: Binomial Nomenclature o Once Linnaeus had decided on a  basis for classifying  organisms , he then  developed a system for naming them . o He gave  each species a name  that consists of  two words.  This system is called binomial nomenclature.  o He  used Latin words  for these names because  all  scientists wrote in Latin in time of Linnaeus . o Thus, the human is  Homo sapiens , and the domestic  (house) cat is  Felis  domesticus .  
---PAGE---
o The  first word  of each name is  called the genus  and the  second word  is called the  species .  o The  genus begins with a capital letter  and the species does  not.  o The  genus and species are either printed in italics or  underlined. Carolus Linnaeus: Binomial Nomenclature 
---PAGE---
Main Classification Groups (Taxa) o There are seven main taxa or classification groups: 1. Species:  Species is a group of individuals that are alike in  many ways and interbreed under natural conditions to  produce fertile offspring (children). 2. Genus:  Genus (plural genera) is a group of species that  are closely similar in structure and evolutionary origin. 3. Family : Family is a group of similar kinds of genera. That  is, similar genera are grouped to form a taxon called  Family. 4. Order:  Similar families are grouped to form a taxon called  order. 
---PAGE---
Main Classification Groups (Taxa) 5. Class:  Similar orders are grouped to form a taxon called  class. 6. Phylum or Division : Similar classes are grouped to form a  taxon called phylum or division.  Zoologists  favour   phylum   and  botanists  favour   division. 7. Kingdom : All the phyla or divisions that contain animals  are grouped in the kingdom  Animalia , and all the phyla or  divisions that contain plants are grouped in the kingdom  Plantae .

Two - Kingdom Classification o Carolus Linnaeus introduced  this system of classification  in 1753.  o This is the oldest and perhaps still the most widely used  system of classification. o   It groups  all organisms into two kingdoms, (1) Plantae ,  which includes bacteria, algae, fungi and higher plants, and  (2) Animalia,  which includes protozoa and higher animals.  o That is, all  living things  are assumed to be  either plants or  animals. 
---PAGE---
Three - Kingdom Classification o Ernst H Haeckel  introduced this system of classification in  1866.  o This system still has the kingdoms ( 1) Plantae and (2)  Animalia , however, it has a  third kingdom ,  (3) Protista .  o The kingdom  Protista contains  all microorganisms,  including the bacteria, blue - green algae, protozoa,  unicellular algae ( eg. ,  flagellales ), and microscopic fungi. o This system was recognized by many biologists during the  first half of the 20 th   century  and is still very popular. 
---PAGE---
Four - Kingdom Classification o To solve the problem with  prokayotic   and eukaryotic  organisms in classification,   Herbert Faulkner Copeland  (1902 -   1968) proposed the four kingdom classification  in  1956.  o The four kingdoms were  Monera, Protista, Plantae, and  Animalia . o He grouped  unicellular organisms   into two large  kingdoms: the  Monera kingdom  and the  Protista kingdom . 
---PAGE---
Four - Kingdom Classification o In 1966, he included  bacteria and  one of the most primitive  algae, called  blue -   green algae , under the Monera kingdom. o The kingdom Monera consists of unicellular organisms. o The kingdoms Protista, Plantae, and Animalia are the  eukaryotic organisms. o The  fungi were placed in the kingdom Plantae  which was  one of the  drawbacks of the four kingdom classification . 
---PAGE---
Five - Kingdom Classification o Robert H Whittaker  introduced this system of classification  in 1969.  o In this system  fungi were separated from the kingdom  Plantae   as they are quite different from the usual plants. o Therefore, the five kingdoms are ( 1) Plantae, (2) Animalia,  (3) Fungi (4) Protista, and (5) Monera . 
---PAGE---
Phylogenetic System Classification o Carl R  Woese   introduced a  completely new approach  in  classification of organisms in 1977 that is  based on  evolutionary (phylogenetic) relationship   among organisms. o Woese   meticulously  analysed   ribosomal ribonucleic acid  (rRNA) , genetic molecules that coordinate part of protein  production.  o Because  rRNA shows only slight variation from one  generation to the next , it is an  excellent tool for revealing the  evolutionary, or family, relationships among organisms . 
---PAGE---
Phylogenetic System Classification o There are three important RNAs occurring in the cells  ΓÇô   rRNA, mRNA, and tRNA (transfer RNA).  o rRNA or ribosomal RNA are molecules found in the cells  involved in the protein synthesis of ribosomes with the help  of mRNA (messenger RNA). o 16S rRNA is a type of rRNA involved in making the small  subunit of the prokaryotic ribosome; these are components  of the 30S subunit (prokaryotic ribosome).  
---PAGE---

---PAGE---
Phylogenetic System Classification o Woese   spent nearly a decade  analysing   the rRNA of various  types of bacteria and arranging them into a microbial  evolutionary tree.  o Use this concept all organisms are now classified into three  kingdoms.  o They are  (1) Archaea, (2) Bacteria, and (3) Eukarya.

The Chemical nature of enzymes Enzymes are  specific:   each enzyme usually  catalyses only  one type of reaction .   Enzymes combine with their substrates to form temporary  enzyme - substrate  complex.   Enzymes are  not altered or used up   by the reactions they  catalyze, so can be used again and again.   Enzymes are   sensitive  to  temperature and  pH .     Many enzymes need  cofactors   in order to function.   Enzyme function may be slowed down or stopped by   inhibitors .  
---PAGE---
Enzyme Classification There are 6 types of reaction they can catalyze - Oxidoreductase : These catalyze oxidation and reduction reactions.  Transferase :   These catalyze the transfer of a chemical group from one  compound to another.  Hydrolase :   These catalyze hydrolysis (splitting by use of water) reactions.      Most digestive enzymes are  hydrolases . Lyase :   these catalyze the break down of molecules by reactions that do not   involved hydrolysis.  Isomerase :   These catalyze the transformation of one isomer into another  Ligase :  These catalyze the formation of bonds between compounds,          often using the free energy made available from ATP hydrolysis.
